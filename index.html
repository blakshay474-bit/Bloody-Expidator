HISTORICAL ANALYSIS

Forensic Serology Through Time

Explore the evolution of forensic serology methodologies, landmark cases, and technological breakthroughs that shaped modern blood evidence analysis.

1901

ABO Blood Group Discovery

Karl Landsteiner discovers ABO blood group system, earning the Nobel Prize. This breakthrough laid the foundation for forensic serology, enabling blood type classification in criminal investigations.

ABO SystemKarl LandsteinerNobel Prize
1915

Precipitin Test Introduction

Leon Lattes develops the precipitin test for distinguishing human from animal blood using antiserum reactions, a technique still used in modern forensics.

Precipitin TestSpecies OriginAntiserum
1925

Secretor Status Discovery

Discovery that approximately 80% of people secrete ABO antigens in body fluids like saliva, semen, and vaginal secretions — crucial for identifying suspects from non-blood evidence.

Secretor StatusBody FluidsAntigen Secretion
1953

Electrophoresis in Forensics

Gel electrophoresis adopted for serum protein and enzyme typing in bloodstain analysis, dramatically increasing the discriminating power of serological evidence.

ElectrophoresisProtein TypingEnzyme Analysis
1984

DNA Fingerprinting Invented

Sir Alec Jeffreys develops DNA fingerprinting at Leicester University, revolutionizing forensic science. First criminal application in the Colin Pitchfork case (1986).

DNA FingerprintingAlec JeffreysRFLP Analysis
1994

STR Profiling Standardized

Short Tandem Repeat (STR) analysis becomes the gold standard for forensic DNA profiling. CODIS database established by FBI for DNA profile matching across jurisdictions.

STR AnalysisCODISFBI Database
2003

Touch DNA & Low Copy Number

Advances in PCR sensitivity enable DNA profiling from trace amounts of biological material — including touch DNA from skin cells on surfaces, expanding evidence collection beyond blood.

Touch DNALCN DNAPCR Amplification
2015

Rapid DNA Technology

Rapid DNA instruments enable full STR profile generation in under 2 hours directly at crime scenes or booking stations, dramatically accelerating forensic serology workflows.

Rapid DNAField AnalysisReal-time Profiling

FLUID ANALYSIS

Body Fluid Correlation Matrix

Analyze the forensic relationships and co-occurrence patterns between various biological fluids found at crime scenes.

Low Correlation
High Correlation

Blood

Most common forensic body fluid. Contains ABO antigens, DNA, and proteins. Found in 78% of violent crime scenes. Analyzed via ABO typing, DNA profiling, and spatter pattern analysis.

Scene Frequency: 78% DNA Yield: High

Saliva

Contains epithelial cells and ABO antigens in secretors. Recovered from bite marks, drinking vessels, stamps, and cigarette butts. Amylase testing confirms presence.

Scene Frequency: 45% DNA Yield: Moderate

Semen

Critical in sexual assault cases. Contains spermatozoa and prostatic acid phosphatase (PAP). Acid phosphatase test used for presumptive identification. High DNA yield from sperm cells.

Scene Frequency: 32% DNA Yield: Very High

Sweat

Contains trace DNA from shed epithelial cells. Found on clothing, tools, and points of contact. Low DNA quantity but useful for touch DNA analysis in absence of other fluids.

Scene Frequency: 58% DNA Yield: Low

Tears

Rarely collected in forensic contexts but may be found on tissues, pillows, or masks. Contains lysozyme and lactoferrin. Limited forensic value but can provide supportive DNA evidence.

Scene Frequency: 8% DNA Yield: Very Low

Vaginal Secretions

Important in sexual assault case work. Contains glycogenated epithelial cells. Lugol's iodine test used for presumptive identification. ABO antigens present in secretors.

Scene Frequency: 18% DNA Yield: Moderate
